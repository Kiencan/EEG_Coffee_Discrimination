## 2. Related Work

### 2.1. EEG and the Neural Processing of Odors

Modern olfactory electrophysiology in humans began with the work of Kobal and colleagues, who developed a flow olfactometer capable of delivering precisely timed pulses of odorant without producing tactile or thermal artefacts on the nasal mucosa [18]. This advance made it possible to record reproducible chemosensory event related potentials (CSERPs) and opened the way for the systematic study of olfactory cortical processing in healthy participants and clinical populations [9]. The canonical CSERP waveform contains an early positive deflection labelled P1, a prominent negative component N1 that peaks roughly between 320 and 450 ms after stimulus onset, and a late positive complex centred around P2 [9]. These components are largest over frontal and central electrodes such as Fz, Cz, and Pz, which sit above the secondary olfactory cortex and the orbitofrontal projection areas.

Beyond the time domain, oscillatory analyses have shown that odors modulate cortical rhythms across the standard EEG bands. Studies using Welch periodograms or time frequency decomposition have reported increases in theta and gamma power during odor delivery, decreases in alpha power that accompany attentional engagement, and changes in beta activity that correlate with hedonic judgement [10], [13], [19]. Gamma activity in particular has been linked to fine grained odor discrimination, consistent with the role of high frequency oscillations in binding distributed neural assemblies [10]. Several groups have also begun to look at functional connectivity, demonstrating that phase synchronisation between frontal, central, and temporal sites can separate odor categories that participants struggle to identify verbally [12]. Taken together, this body of work establishes that odor identity, intensity, and pleasantness leave detectable signatures in both event related and oscillatory EEG, providing the empirical foundation for data driven decoding approaches.

A complementary line of research has examined the temporal dynamics of odor representation at the population level. Iravani and colleagues recently applied multivariate decoding to high density EEG and reported that pairwise discrimination between odors rises above chance approximately 100 ms after odor onset and reaches its maximum around 350 ms, with information distributed over a broad fronto temporal network [16]. Their results support the view that meaningful odor specific information is available in scalp EEG within the first half second of stimulus onset, which is precisely the window covered by the three second sniff epochs used in the present study.

### 2.2. Machine Learning for EEG Signal Classification

Machine learning has become the standard tool for translating raw EEG into category labels in brain computer interface research. The classical pipeline extracts hand crafted features such as band power, spectral entropy, or autoregressive coefficients from short epochs, and feeds them to a linear or kernel classifier, most commonly linear discriminant analysis, logistic regression, or support vector machines [17]. For motor imagery and related paradigms, the common spatial pattern (CSP) algorithm of Ramoser and colleagues remains a strong baseline by learning spatial filters that maximise the variance ratio between two classes [20]. More recently, Riemannian geometry approaches that treat trial covariance matrices as points on the manifold of symmetric positive definite matrices have produced state of the art results in several BCI competitions and offer attractive transfer properties across sessions and subjects [21].

Deep learning has reshaped the field over the past decade. EEGNet, introduced by Lawhern and colleagues, is a compact convolutional network that combines temporal, depthwise, and separable convolutions to capture spatial and spectral structure with only a few thousand parameters, and it has been validated across a wide range of paradigms including P300, error related negativity, and sensorimotor rhythms [15]. Schirrmeister and colleagues proposed deep and shallow ConvNet variants that match or exceed filter bank CSP on motor imagery decoding when trained with cropped sampling and modern regularisation [22]. These architectures have since been adapted for emotion recognition, sleep staging, and, to a lesser extent, olfactory tasks.

A persistent challenge in EEG decoding is the gap between within subject and across subject performance. Lotte and colleagues, in their decade spanning review of EEG classification algorithms, emphasise that random trial splits within a single recording overestimate generalisation, and recommend leave one subject out (LOSO) or cross session evaluation whenever the goal is a calibration free system [17]. Recent benchmarks confirm that LOSO accuracy is typically much lower than within session accuracy because models must cope with anatomical, electrode, and behavioural differences between participants [23]. A growing body of work therefore investigates transfer learning, domain adaptation, and Riemannian alignment as ways to close this gap, but the underlying problem of cross subject generalisation remains unsolved in many EEG paradigms [23]. These methodological considerations directly motivate the strict LOSO protocol adopted in the present study.

### 2.3. EEG Studies on Food, Fragrance, and Coffee Aroma

A growing body of applied EEG work has examined how everyday odors and food stimuli shape brain activity. Reviews of fragrance research summarise dozens of experiments showing that essential oils such as lavender, peppermint, and rosemary produce reproducible shifts in alpha and beta power, often interpreted as changes in arousal and relaxation [24]. Park and colleagues have used similar protocols to compare cosmetic fragrances and report differences in frontal asymmetry that align with subjective pleasantness ratings [24]. While these studies establish that everyday odors elicit measurable EEG responses, they typically rely on group level statistics rather than trial level classification, and they rarely report cross subject generalisation.

Closer to the present work, several recent papers have attempted single trial odor classification from scalp EEG. Kroupi and colleagues released the Odor Pleasantness Perception Database and trained support vector machines on band power features to discriminate pleasant from unpleasant odors at the trial level, reporting accuracies well above chance for within subject evaluation [13]. Hou and colleagues extended this line of work with a convolutional architecture that operates on band specific spectral features and reported high accuracy on an eight class odor identification task, again under within subject training [11]. Hou and colleagues subsequently applied an olfactory EEG pipeline to food identification and pleasure analysis, showing that food related odors evoke patterns distinguishable from non food stimuli [14]. In parallel, the gustatory community has explored EEG based classification of basic tastes, with recent CNN attention hybrids exceeding ninety percent accuracy on within subject evaluation of sweet, sour, bitter, salty, umami, and neutral stimuli [25]. These studies confirm that EEG carries information about complex chemosensory stimuli, but most of them rely on within subject splits and contrast highly dissimilar stimuli.

Coffee aroma specifically has received less attention. A small number of studies have measured EEG during coffee inhalation and reported that the smell of coffee produces arousing effects on alpha and beta activity, in contrast to the calming pattern observed for lavender [24]. A recent investigation on thirty young adults found that coffee aroma altered cerebral activity during concentration tasks and modulated prefrontal theta and beta power [26]. However, to the best of our knowledge no published work has directly addressed the binary classification of *Coffea arabica* against *Coffea canephora* from scalp EEG at the single trial level, and no study has reported subject independent results for this contrast. The present paper is intended to fill this gap by combining a controlled olfactory protocol, a transparent quality controlled processing pipeline, and a strict LOSO evaluation, and by reporting honest results that include both the strengths and the limitations of classical band power features for this particularly difficult discrimination problem.

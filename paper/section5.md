## 5. Discussion

### 5.1. Interpretation of the Main Finding

The headline result of this study is that, under a strict subject independent evaluation, neither classical machine learning models on spectral band power features nor a range of alternative representations could discriminate the aroma of *Coffea arabica* from that of *Coffea canephora* above the empirical chance ceiling. The best random forest reached an accuracy of 0.546 with an AUC of 0.553, and the engineered feature superset together with a linear SVM peaked at a balanced accuracy of 0.538. These numbers are very close to the chance baseline and do not survive a within subject permutation test. The same ceiling appeared in the coffee versus air contrast and in the regression of the participants' own valence, intensity, and preference ratings, which indicates that the bottleneck does not lie in the choice of label but in the amount of subject invariant olfactory information available in the recorded signal.

This outcome is consistent with the broader pattern in the EEG odor decoding literature. The studies that report high single trial accuracies for odor classification almost invariably evaluate within subject splits and contrast highly dissimilar odorants such as a pleasant flower against an unpleasant chemical [11], [13], [14]. When evaluation moves to a cross subject regime or to within category contrasts between two members of the same product family, accuracies drop sharply and often approach chance, as documented in cross subject benchmarks of EEG decoding more generally [17], [23]. The present results extend this picture to the specific case of coffee aroma and confirm that two roasted coffee species, which share a large fraction of their volatile inventory and are confused by untrained humans, sit at the difficult end of the EEG decoding spectrum.

### 5.2. Why a Transparent Data Quality Audit Mattered

The sensitivity analysis on the full eighteen subject pool offers a methodological lesson that we believe is at least as important as the main classification result. Including the eleven clipped recordings raised random forest accuracy from 0.546 to approximately 0.58 and AUC from 0.553 to 0.61, an improvement that could easily be reported as a positive result by a less cautious pipeline. A finer inspection revealed, however, that this gain was driven by the structured prevalence of clipping in subjects P003 to P013 rather than by olfactory cortical responses. Hardware saturation produces non linear distortion that injects harmonic content across all standard EEG bands and, crucially, varies systematically by participant, which exposes a per subject fingerprint that the LOSO classifier can exploit instead of the intended odor label.

This observation echoes a recurring theme in the EEG literature: subject specific confounders, whether they come from impedance drift, electrode location, sweating, or in our case amplifier clipping, can inflate cross subject accuracy even when no genuine task related signal is present [17]. Publication of negative or chance level results, accompanied by a transparent quality audit that quantifies such confounders, is therefore important for the credibility of EEG based aroma decoding. Our pipeline embeds this audit as a non optional first stage and releases the per subject saturation counts so that any reader can reproduce both the exclusion criterion and the resulting cohort.

### 5.3. Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged when interpreting the present results. The most consequential is the modest acquisition setup. The KT88 amplifier operates at 100 Hz with sixteen channels, which restricts spectral analysis to frequencies below the Nyquist limit of 50 Hz and provides limited spatial sampling of the scalp. Higher density montages with 64 or 128 channels would allow source level analyses and a finer characterisation of frontal and temporal contributions known to be relevant for olfactory processing [9], [16]. A faster sampling rate, in the range typically used for chemosensory ERP research, would also enable reliable independent component analysis for the removal of ocular and muscular artefacts, which were not separately controlled in this study. The amplifier full scale of ±204.8 µV is another factor: in retrospect, the over amplification settings used for participants P003 to P013 produced systematic clipping that could have been avoided by a more conservative gain calibration.

Second, the olfactory protocol relied on natural coffee samples delivered manually by the experimenter rather than on calibrated pulses from an olfactometer. Manual delivery is ecologically valid and matches the everyday experience of smelling coffee, but it introduces variability in onset timing and concentration that an automated olfactometer of the Kobal type would eliminate [9], [18]. This jitter is benign for hedonic and arousal analyses but may obscure the precise temporal locking of CSERPs that early decoding work depends on [16].

Third, the sample size of eight clean participants is small. Although this is not unusual for olfactory EEG studies, it limits statistical power and leaves the cross subject generalisation claim with a wide confidence interval. A replication on a larger cohort with a cleaner acquisition chain would be needed before drawing strong conclusions about the absolute difficulty of the Arabica versus Robusta discrimination.

Finally, our analysis stopped at classical machine learning on spectral features. Although the feature search covered a broad space and a PCA baseline confirmed that richer representations did not exceed the same ceiling, we did not train end to end deep architectures on the present dataset, primarily because the small sample size and the modest electrode count make convergence of such models unreliable.

### 5.4. Implications and Future Directions

These results suggest several directions that are likely to be more productive than further iteration on band power features. The most immediate is a shift from subject independent to within subject evaluation. The within subject regime has produced strong odor classification results in past work [13], [14], and a personalised model trained on a few minutes of calibration data per user may be sufficient for applications such as expert training, quality control assistance, or consumer feedback systems. Within subject pipelines also offer a more direct path to understanding which spectral or temporal features carry coffee specific information for individual brains, which can then guide group level generalisation in later studies.

A second promising direction is the systematic use of event related features. The present pipeline averaged spectral content across the full three second sniff epoch, which integrates over the early CSERP components and over later evaluation related activity. Time resolved analyses of N1, P2, and the late positive complex, combined with sniff aligned epoching, would test whether the early olfactory cortex response carries discriminative information about coffee species even when the broadband spectral profile does not [9], [16], [19].

A third direction is the inclusion of functional connectivity features. Phase based measures such as phase lag index or weighted phase lag index between frontal, central, and temporal electrodes have been reported to distinguish odor categories in previous work and may be more robust to amplitude differences between participants than band power [12]. Combined with Riemannian alignment of trial covariance matrices, connectivity based representations would be a natural fit for the LOSO setting and connect directly to the methodological tools that have proven successful in motor imagery and emotion BCIs [21].

A fourth and more ambitious direction is end to end deep learning. Architectures such as EEGNet have shown that compact convolutional networks can learn discriminative spatial and temporal filters directly from raw signals across a variety of paradigms [15], and deeper variants tailored to EEG decoding have reached or exceeded the performance of filter bank CSP on motor imagery [22]. Pre training such models on larger public EEG datasets and fine tuning on the present cohort, possibly with domain adaptation between subjects, would be a natural next step. Combining EEG with synchronised respiration traces or galvanic skin response would further enrich the multimodal description of the olfactory event and may compensate for the limited spatial resolution of the current acquisition setup.

Finally, future studies could benefit from a more controlled olfactory protocol that pairs natural coffee samples with calibrated reference odorants of known chemical composition. This design would allow direct comparison between within category (Arabica versus Robusta) and between category (coffee versus reference) discrimination on the same participants, providing a stronger empirical handle on the difficulty gradient that the present paper has only begun to chart.

Taken together, the limitations and the directions above outline a research programme in which the present study serves as a baseline, a transparent reference point, and a methodological template. We hope that the honest reporting of chance level results, supported by a reproducible pipeline and a documented data quality audit, will be useful both for groups that wish to replicate or extend the experiment and for the broader EEG olfactory community that is moving toward more ecologically valid stimuli such as coffee.

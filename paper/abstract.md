EEG-Based Coffee Aroma Discrimination Using Machine Learning to Decode Brain Responses to Olfactory Stimuli

Abstract:
Coffee aroma strongly shapes consumer preference and market value, yet sensory evaluation by trained panels is subjective, costly, and tiring, while electronic noses capture chemistry but not how the brain perceives aroma. This study tested whether electroencephalography (EEG) with machine learning can distinguish Arabica from Robusta coffee aromas, separate coffee from odorless air, and relate neural activity to subjective ratings. Using a low channel clinical device with 16 EEG channels at 100 Hz, we recorded a passive smelling task of 45 trials per person (15 Arabica, 15 Robusta, 15 odorless control) from 18 of 20 recruited students who rarely drink coffee. After filtering, common average referencing, and artifact rejection, we computed band power and related features and classified them with logistic regression, support vector machines, and random forests under subject independent leave one subject out validation. Hardware over amplification clipped the signal in 10 participants, leaving eight clean recordings. On clean data, both the Arabica versus Robusta and the coffee versus air classification stayed near chance, with best balanced accuracy about 0.55, and EEG features did not reliably predict pleasantness, intensity, or preference. The only weak link was higher temporal theta power with greater pleasantness. We provide the first Arabica versus Robusta EEG dataset, a reproducible pipeline, and an honest account of data quality limits.
Keywords: EEG, Olfactory Perception, Coffee Aroma, Machine Learning, Brain-Computer Interface, Sensory Evaluation.
